# Chapter 1: It Didn't Just Happen

---

The first time I sat across from Grand Master Nan Lu, I didn't know what I was walking into.

I had been dealing with chronic fatigue for years — not tiredness, but the deeper thing, the kind that doesn't respond to rest, the kind that has settled into the body like weather. Along with it: headaches that came without warning, migraines that could take out an entire day, and a persistent pressure in my jaw that I had stopped noticing the way you stop noticing a hum in the walls. I had been to doctors. I had tried the protocols. I had read the books on adrenal fatigue, on inflammation, on nervous system dysregulation. I had a shelf of supplements and a list of things I was supposed to avoid. Nothing had resolved. Things had been managed, treated, controlled — never actually fixed.

A friend told me about Grand Master Nan Lu. She said he was a master of Traditional Chinese Medicine who had been practicing and teaching for decades, that people traveled across the country to see him. I didn't know what that meant, exactly. I had never sat with a practitioner of this tradition before. I wasn't sure what to expect — some combination of herbs, probably, and a different vocabulary for the same diagnosis I'd already received in other offices.

What he said to me was not what I was expecting.

---

He looked at me for a while. Not the way a doctor looks at you — cataloguing, cross-referencing — but something quieter than that. Like he was reading something he'd seen before and was taking his time with it.

Then he said: "You are not flexible."

I thought he meant my body. I had always been tight — the kind of person who fails the sit-and-reach test and has a physical therapist who sighs when reviewing their posture. I was about to agree when he said something that stopped me.

"Not your body. Your mind. You can only see one way. You must go that one way. This is causing all of your problems."

I sat with that for a moment. The headaches. The fatigue. The jaw. He was saying they were downstream of something in the mind — not psychological in the way that phrase usually dismisses, but structurally. A posture in the way I processed the world, in the way I moved through choices and commitments and obstacles. And that posture, held long enough, had expressed itself in the body. Literally. Physically. In specific, traceable locations.

The word that people who knew me had always used was *driven*. Or *focused*. Or, occasionally, *stubborn*. I had treated those as synonyms for the same quality — a quality that had served me well by most measures. Grand Master Lu was describing the same quality. He was calling it a trap.

---

I asked him how I ended up here.

He said: "You learned all the wrong things."

Not that I had failed to learn. That I had learned very well — and what I had learned was wrong.

At some point in that conversation, he gestured toward the walls of his office. I looked around and realized something I hadn't noticed when I walked in: there was nothing on them. No diplomas. No certifications. No framed credentials, no awards, no institutional seals. In the kind of professional office I was used to visiting, the walls are covered in evidence — degrees from recognizable schools, licenses from recognizable bodies, proof that someone sanctioned by the system has reviewed this person and found them sufficient. His walls were bare.

He said: "I don't need any of that."

Not with arrogance. As a simple statement of fact, the way you'd say you don't need an umbrella when you're already inside. Then he looked back at me and said something that landed like a key turning in a lock I hadn't known was there.

"You are always chasing the A grade. That is what is exhausting you."

I have thought about that sentence more than almost anything else he has said to me. Because it was not about school. It was about the entire architecture of how I moved through my life. The relentless need to get it right, to be assessed and found sufficient, to perform well enough for whatever standard I had absorbed from whatever environment I was in. The A grade doesn't stay in the classroom. It follows you into every room. Into every project, every relationship, every decision about how to spend an afternoon. It becomes the hidden criterion for everything — and because it is always slightly out of reach, always one more effort away, it never stops demanding.

That is not drive. That is a machine that doesn't have an off switch.

I have thought about that sentence almost every week since he said it. Because it named something I had never been able to name. The things I had been taught about how to move through the world — focus on a goal and do not deviate, push through resistance, commit fully or not at all, have a plan and execute it, do not show uncertainty — these were not random personal habits. They were lessons. Taught deliberately, reinforced continuously, rewarded publicly. I had learned them from people who believed they were correct. From a culture that treated them as virtues. I had internalized them so completely that I had stopped being able to see them as a choice. They had become the only way I knew how to move.

That is the thing about wrong learning. It doesn't feel like a mistake. It feels like you.

---

Here is the thing that Traditional Chinese Medicine sees that most of what we call wellness does not.

The body and the mind are not two systems loosely connected by metaphor. They are one. What happens in the mind is expressed in the body — not as a side effect, not as a secondary consequence, but as a direct translation. The pattern of the mind becomes the condition of the body. Mental rigidity — the inability to consider more than one path, the compulsion to force through, the refusal to yield — produces physical constriction. In specific locations. With remarkable consistency. Across all kinds of people, across all kinds of lives.

The jaw, where tension concentrates when you are gripping something you will not release. The head, when the pressure of a single relentless direction has nowhere to go. The neck and shoulders, where the accumulated force of a life that does not know how to stop becomes permanent armor.

These are not poetic associations. They are observations — the kind built from thousands of patients, over hundreds of years, by practitioners who were trying to understand not just what was broken but *how it got that way*. That question — how did you get here — is the question that the tradition begins with. It is also the question that most of what I had tried before had never thought to ask.

Grand Master Lu asked it in the first ten minutes.

---

What opened up in that room was not what I had expected to find.

I had come looking for a new treatment. A different diagnosis, maybe — a more refined version of the same story I had been told in other offices. What I got instead was a different kind of question entirely: *what have I been taught that made me this way?* Not "what is wrong with me?" — a search that ends at a purchase — but "where did this come from, and can I learn something different?"

Those are not the same investigation. One is a transaction. The other is a reckoning.

The curiosity that opened in that first meeting was, I understand now, the first genuine movement toward health I had made in years. Not because I had found an answer, but because I had found the right question. What does it mean to not be flexible? Where did I learn to see only one way? What would it actually look like to change?

And underneath all of that: if the patterns running my life were not innate but acquired — if I had learned them from a world that was confidently wrong — then what would it mean to learn something different?

That is what this book is about. Not a treatment. A different way of seeing.

---

"You are not flexible." Three words that reframed everything I thought I knew about the problem I was carrying. Not a deficiency. Not bad luck. Not something that had happened to me. A posture. One I had learned. One I could, in theory, unlearn.

I didn't know, sitting in that room, how long that road was. I only knew that I had heard something true, in a way I hadn't heard in any of the offices before that one.

Call it *the wrong learning*. The particular way each of us has been educated into a pattern that the body is faithfully executing, year after year, until the signals become impossible to ignore.

Your body is not malfunctioning. It is doing exactly what it was taught to do.

The question is whether you're ready to learn something different.
