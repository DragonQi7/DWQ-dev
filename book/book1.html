Chapter 1

The Wrong Learning

An excerpt from Still Tired: Why You Got Here, What Your Body Has Been Trying to Tell You, and What It Actually Takes to Change

The last thing you want, when you've been exhausted for years, is to be told that you caused it.

I didn't realize that's what he was saying. Not at first.

I had spent over a decade at one of the world's largest investment banks. The hours were not just long — they were total. The culture was built on a simple, unstated equation: the more you gave, the more you were worth. Nobody told you to skip sleep. Nobody had to. The work was always there, the deal was always moving, and the unspoken rule was that the person who went home first was the one who cared least. I believed in the system completely. I had been trained to. And for a long time, by every visible metric, the system believed in me back.

What I didn't see — what I couldn't see, from inside it — was what it was costing.

The symptoms didn't arrive all at once. They accumulated, the way debt does, invisibly, until the balance is suddenly impossible to ignore.

First it was the sleep. Not dramatic insomnia — not lying awake for hours in distress. Just the gradual erosion of depth. I was sleeping, technically, but waking up already running. The rest was there, but it wasn't landing. Over time I stopped expecting it to.

Then the headaches. Then the migraines. The kind that don't just hurt — they shut you down. A day gone. Then two. And still the work was there, waiting.

Then vertigo. The first time it happened I was standing in a meeting room on the forty-third floor, looking out at the city, and the floor tilted. Not dramatically — just enough to make me reach for the table. I told no one. I filed it away.

Then the jaw. A constant low-grade pressure that I couldn't locate precisely, couldn't explain, and eventually stopped noticing the way you stop noticing a hum in the walls of a building you've worked in long enough.

Then the labs started coming back with numbers in the wrong direction. Pre-diabetic range. Inflammation markers elevated. The kind of results a doctor circles in red and calls a warning, that you absorb intellectually and continue doing exactly what you were doing.

And somewhere underneath all of it — quieter, harder to name, easier to dismiss — anxiety. Depression. Not the clinical dramatic kind. The functional kind: getting everything done, keeping up the performance, and feeling, underneath it, like the floor was always slightly tilted.

I had a hypothesis for all of it. I was a person who dealt in data, frameworks, and evidence-based reasoning. I had read the literature on cortisol dysregulation, on HPA axis function, on the relationship between sleep deprivation and metabolic disruption. I understood, conceptually, what chronic stress does to a body. I had built a model of my own condition that was coherent, referenced, and completely useless at making any of it stop.

That is the part I couldn't explain. I understood the mechanism. I couldn't interrupt it.

I had been to doctors. Done the protocols. Built a shelf of supplements organized by evidence quality and a list of interventions ranked by effect size. I had tried the things with the strongest research support and the things at the fringes. Nothing had fixed it. Things had been managed, treated, sustained at a level I had quietly accepted as the new baseline.

Then a friend told me about Grand Master Nan Lu.

She said he was a master of Traditional Chinese Medicine — that he had been practicing and teaching for decades, that people traveled across the country to see him. I was skeptical in the specific way that analytically trained people are skeptical of things that don't arrive with a peer-reviewed citation: not hostile, but waiting. I had no framework for what he did. I didn't know how to evaluate it. I went anyway, because my framework hadn't been working.

He looked at me for a long moment after I sat down. Not the way a doctor looks at you — scanning, categorizing, building a case — but something quieter. Like he had seen this before and was taking his time with it.

He said: "You are not flexible."

I thought he meant my body. I was about to agree when he stopped me.

"Not your body. Your mind. You can only see one way. This is causing all of your problems."

Here is what the analytical mind does with a statement like that.

It searches for the mechanism. It wants to know the causal pathway, the confounding variables, the study design. It wants the claim supported before it will grant the claim any weight. That is not a flaw — that discipline has produced most of what we know about medicine. But it has a blind spot, and the blind spot is this: it can only ask questions that fit inside its own framework. When the evidence is right in front of you — in your own body, in your own daily experience — and the framework still can't explain it, the honest next step is not to defend the framework. It's to ask what else might be true.

I had been asking my body questions in one language for years. The answers kept coming back in a language I didn't recognize. That morning, for the first time, someone was offering to translate.

What this tradition sees differently is not the body. It's the word separate.

The mind and body, in this view, are not two systems coordinated by metaphor. They are one. A pattern in the mind doesn't cause symptoms as a secondary consequence — it becomes them. Mental rigidity — the inability to consider more than one path, the compulsion to push through rather than yield — produces physical constriction. In specific locations. With remarkable consistency. Across all kinds of people, across all kinds of lives.

The jaw, where you hold what you will not release. The head, where the pressure of one relentless direction has nowhere to go. The neck and shoulders, where the accumulated force of a life that doesn't know how to stop becomes permanent armor.

I had the jaw. I had the head. I had the shoulders.

These are not poetic associations. They are observations — built from thousands of patients, over hundreds of years, by practitioners asking one question before any other: how did you get here? Not "what is your diagnosis?" That question comes later, if at all. The first question is how. What is the pattern that produced this? What is the life inside which this condition makes sense?

That question is where this tradition begins. It was also the question that none of the offices I'd been to had ever thought to ask.

Grand Master Lu asked it in the first ten minutes.

At some point in that conversation, he gestured toward the walls of his office.

I looked up and realized I hadn't noticed: there was nothing on them. No diplomas. No certifications. No framed proof that some institution had reviewed him and found him sufficient. In every professional context I had ever occupied, the walls were covered in credentials — not because they proved competence, but because they signaled belonging. They were the receipts of the system. My own office wall had its version of the same display. I had never questioned it.

His walls were bare.

"I don't need any of that," he said.

Not with arrogance. The way you'd say you don't need an umbrella when you're already inside. Then he looked back at me and said the thing that landed like a key turning in a lock I hadn't known was there.

"You are always chasing the A grade. That is what is exhausting you."

That sentence has stayed with me because it was not about school.

The A grade doesn't live in the classroom. It follows you. Into every project, every relationship, every quarterly review, every quiet Sunday when you sit down to rest and can't quite let yourself. It becomes the hidden criterion for everything — always one more effort away, never fully arrived at, always slightly out of reach. The people I had worked alongside at the bank were wired exactly this way. We called it high performance. We called it excellence culture. We competed for who could sustain it longest. None of us had a name for what it cost.

Most people wired this way think of it as drive. The word I heard most often applied to me was driven, and I had always accepted it as a compliment. Grand Master Lu was describing the same quality. He was calling it a machine with no off switch.

And here is the part that has taken years to absorb: I hadn't invented that machine. I had been taught to build it. The relentless forward motion, the inability to yield, the need to perform well enough for whatever standard the room was holding — these were lessons. Taught deliberately, reinforced continuously, rewarded publicly. I had learned them from people who believed they were correct, from a culture that treated them as virtues. I had internalized them so completely that I had stopped being able to see them as a choice. They had become the only way I knew how to move.

That is the particular cruelty of wrong learning. It doesn't feel like a mistake. It feels like you.

What I couldn't reconcile, sitting in that room, was this: the system that had failed me was the most rigorous one I knew. I had applied the best available evidence to my own condition. I had been methodical, thorough, disciplined — all the qualities that the analytical world rewards. And I was still fatigued, still in pain, still tilting vertigo-style toward a metabolic breakdown that the data said was coming.

Meanwhile, here was a man with bare walls and a vocabulary I didn't share, looking at me and saying things that were, somehow, more accurate than anything a specialist with framed credentials had offered.

That tension did not resolve cleanly. It still hasn't. What I came to understand — slowly, over many months — is that the two frameworks were not competing explanations for the same thing. They were asking different questions. The framework I had come from asked: what is wrong, and how do we fix it? The framework in that room asked: what is the pattern, and where did it come from? The first question looks for a malfunction. The second looks for a meaning. Neither is complete without the other. But if you've only ever asked the first one, you will eventually hit a wall — the wall I had hit — where the fixing keeps happening and the condition keeps returning, because the pattern that produces it was never addressed.

That morning was the first time the second question had been asked of me.

What this tradition sees, in the particular cluster of symptoms I was carrying — the fatigue, the insomnia, the headaches, the jaw, the metabolic drift — is not a collection of independent breakdowns. It sees a single pattern expressing itself through multiple channels. The body is not misbehaving. It is doing exactly what the pattern asks of it, faithfully, year after year, until the signals become impossible to ignore.

That single pattern, in my case, had a name. Inflexibility. Not in the muscles. In the mind.

And inflexibility of mind — the inability to yield, to consider more than one path, to stop when the body says stop — has a precise physical expression. The jaw grips. The head pressures. The sleep never restores, because a mind that can't yield at midnight can't restore at three in the morning either. The blood sugar dysregulates because the cortisol doesn't stop. The anxiety arrives because the system knows something is wrong even when the mind refuses to. The depression, underneath it all, is the body's last resort: a forced slowdown when no other signal has worked.

None of this is poetic. It is a chain. And Traditional Chinese Medicine — a tradition with over two thousand years of clinical observation behind it — traces that chain back to its source.

This book is about what I learned once I started following that chain.

Chapter by chapter, it maps a path out of the condition I was in — but more than that, it maps the framework required to walk that path. Because the insight in that room was only the beginning. Understanding that the problem was wrong learning did not automatically undo the learning. That work is slower, more layered, and more demanding than anything the analytical world had asked of me. Not because it is hard in the brute-force sense — not like the sixty-hour weeks and the quarterly sprints — but because it requires something different: a new quality of attention, sustained over a long time, applied to a body and a mind that you have spent years training not to listen to each other.

The path through fatigue, burnout, and chronic symptoms is real. It exists. It has worked for people for millennia — not as a lucky accident, but as a coherent system built on principles that hold across different bodies, different lives, different kinds of wrong learning. But the path has requirements. It requires both quantity and quality of effort: the consistency to show up and the depth to actually arrive. It requires learning to read a framework that does not reward the analytical mind's favorite move, which is to understand everything quickly and move on. This framework rewards something slower and more uncomfortable: the willingness to be a student for a long time.

The chapters ahead are about what that actually looks like. How to find the right entry point for your specific symptoms. What quality of effort means in practice — and how different it is from what high performance culture trained you to call effort. What it means to truly learn a new framework, rather than just understand it. And what changes when the body and the mind finally start speaking the same language again.

I did not walk out of that first meeting fixed. I walked out with a question I hadn't had before.

That was more valuable.

At some point in our first conversation, he gestured toward the walls. I looked up. Nothing there. No degrees, no seals, no evidence that the machine had approved of him. I had spent my entire career in rooms where the walls said: this person has been vetted, this person belongs, this person knows the rules. His walls said nothing.

"I don't need any of that," he said. Then: "You are always chasing the A grade. That is what is exhausting you."

Three words before that. Three words that mattered more.

You are not flexible.

They didn't condemn anything. They didn't prescribe anything. They simply named a pattern that had been running so long it had stopped feeling like a pattern and started feeling like a personality. That is what named things do. They take something that has felt like weather — constant, impersonal, inescapable — and show you the door it came through. If you came through a door, you can leave through one too.

I didn't know, sitting in that room, how long that road was. I only knew that I had heard something true, in a way I hadn't heard in any of the offices that came before.

Call it the wrong learning. The particular education each of us has received — in how to work, how to strive, how to hold ourselves together — that the body has been faithfully executing, year after year, until the signals become impossible to ignore.

Your body is not malfunctioning. It is doing exactly what it was taught to do.

The question is whether you're ready to learn something different.

Before Chapter 2: Think about the last time you forced something through. Not a crisis — just a normal week. A plan that wasn't going right, a decision that took twice as long as it should have, a moment when you overrode what your body was signaling. Where did you feel it? Chest. Jaw. Head. Shoulders. Write the location. One word is enough. That word is the beginning of reading the pattern.
